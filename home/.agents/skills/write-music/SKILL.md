# Write Music

Write for the ear. Make sentence length follow meaning so the prose develops a
pulse, builds energy, and knows when to stop.

## Compose

1. Draft for meaning. Preserve every required fact, the requested voice, and the
   reader's level of knowledge.
2. Compose the cadence. Use short sentences to strike, turn, or resolve; medium
   sentences to carry the thought; and long sentences to gather detail, build
   momentum through connected clauses, and land on the strongest idea.
3. Audit the draft's silhouette as short, medium, and long sentences—roughly up
   to 12 words, 13–20 words, and 21 or more. For prose over 100 words, use all
   three registers and create at least one sharp adjacent contrast. Give each
   paragraph its own arc and a clean landing. Let repeated shapes remain when
   their recurrence intensifies the point.
4. Tune by ear. Read the prose silently once at speaking pace. Revise mechanical
   transitions, breathless clauses, long sentences whose energy fades before
   they land, and runs of similar length or syntax that lack a rhythmic purpose.

Finish when every required idea remains, the requested form and length still
hold, all three registers appear in prose over 100 words, each paragraph lands,
every remaining run is deliberate, and each conspicuously short or long sentence
has a rhetorical job. Rhythm should carry the meaning, not compete with it.
